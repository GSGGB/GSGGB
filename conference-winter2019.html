What's going on?

The Global Society for Genetics and Genome Biology (GSGGB) welcomes you to our 2nd annual research conference: Horizons in Molecular Medicine. The aim of our conference this year is to shed light on the pioneering work in molecular medicine research done in Toronto, with an emphasis on genome editing. This is achieved by bringing together world-renowned experts in various sub-fields of basic research.

Breakfast, lunch and beverages are included.



Conference Details

Date: Saturday, March 30, 2019
Time: 8:30 AM - 4:00 PM
Location: University of Toronto Medical Sciences Building (MSB), Room 2158

Dr. Cynthia Hawkins

Neuropathology and Paediatric Laboratory Medicine, Sick Kids; University of Toronto

Dr. Cynthia Hawkins

Neuropathology and Paediatric Laboratory Medicine, Sick Kids; University of Toronto

Dr. Cynthia Hawkins, MD, PhD, is a pediatric neuropathologist at The Hospital for Sick Children. Her practice involves both surgical and autopsy neuropathology and includes neuro-oncologic, neuromuscular and neurodevelopmental disease.

Dr. Hawkins obtained her PhD in 1996 and her MD in 1997 from the University of Western Ontario. She completed her residency training in neuropathology at the University of Toronto in 2002, including a post-doctoral fellowship at the University of Zurich. Dr. Hawkins joined The Hospital for Sick Children as a neuropathologist in 2002 and has been appointed at the Research Institute in the Cell Biology program since 2004. She is a principal investigator at the Arthur and Sonia Labatt Brain Tumour Research Centre and a Professor at the University of Toronto.

Her research interests include:

  • Molecular pathogenesis and therapeutics for pediatric astrocytomas, in particular, diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma (DIPG).
  • Identification and clinical implementation of novel prognostic and therapeutic markers for pediatric brain tumors.

Dr. Kerry Bowman

Clinical Ethicist, Mount Sinai Hospital; University of Toronto

Dr. Kerry Bowman

Clinical Ethicist, Mount Sinai Hospital; University of Toronto

Dr. Kerry Bowman, PhD, is a Canadian bioethicist and conservationist. Born in Montreal and now based in Toronto, he is a frequent public speaker both nationally and internationally. He is best known for his wide-ranging work in the areas of bioethics and environmental protection.

In the field of clinical ethics, Dr. Bowman specializes in end-of-life decision-making and cross-cultural healthcare delivery, as well as genetics, genomics, cloning-animal ethics and ethical questions in emerging medical technologies. He has been consulted on these topics in countries such as Iran, the People’s Republic of China and South Africa.

Dr. Bowman is the clinical ethicist for Mount Sinai Hospital in Toronto and holds an academic appointment with The University of Toronto in Family and Community Medicine. He also serves with The University of Toronto Joint Centre for Bioethics and The University of Toronto Centre for Environment.

Dr. Ren-Ke Li

Cardiovascular Surgery, University of Toronto; Senior Scientist, UHN

Dr. Ren-Ke Li

Cardiovascular Surgery, University of Toronto; Senior Scientist, UHN

Dr. Ren-Ke Li, MD, PhD is a Professor of Medicine in the Department of Surgery, Division of Cardiac Surgery at the University of Toronto. Dr. Li is a Senior Scientist at the Toronto General Research Institute, University Health Network working in the field of stem cell transplantation and tissue engineering. Dr. Li is the Canada Research Chair in Cardiac Regeneration (Tier 1) of the Canadian Institutes of Health Research and was a Career Investigator of the Heart and Stroke Foundation of Canada.

Dr. Li has been at the forefront in the field of cell transplantation and tissue engineering. In 1996, he published the first demonstration that cells transplanted into the injured heart can form muscle tissue, regenerated damaged hear, and improved heart function. Recently, his group has defined optimal cell types for transplantation and described the optimal conditions under which the transplanted cells can achieve the most efficient repair. Currently, his research group is attempting to rejuvenate aged individual using young stem cells to improve repair and regenerative capacity of aged patients. He also developed a platform for targeted gene or regenerative factor delivery for targeted tissue/organ therapy.



Dr. Peter Roy

Canada Research Chair in Chemical Genetics, University of Toronto

Dr. Peter Roy

Canada Research Chair in Chemical Genetics, University of Toronto

Dr. Peter Roy, PhD, earned his B.Sc. from Dalhousie University (1993) and his Ph.D. from the University of Toronto (1999). From 1999 to 2002, Dr. Roy learned high-throughput genomic skills as a Beckman Post-Doctoral Fellow at Stanford University. Since starting his lab, his group has made pioneering discoveries about how cells and cell-extensions are guided to their target destination during animal development. He was also one of the first to exploit the nematode C. elegans as a high-throughput platform for the discovery of novel small molecule tools and potential drug-leads. Dr. Roy received tenure in 2007 and became a Full Professor in 2012.

Dr. Alan Davidson

Molecular Genetics and Microbiology, University of Toronto

Dr. Alan Davidson

Molecular Genetics and Microbiology, University of Toronto

Dr. Alan Davidson, PhD, earned his BSc and PhD from the University of Toronto and completed a postdoctoral fellowship at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. His research focuses on bacteriophages (phages), the viruses that infect bacteria. His group studies how phages work, and how they can exploit phage-derived entities for applications in human health. His group also studies anti-CRISPRs, which are phage-encoded inhibitors of CRISPR-Cas systems. His lab has discovered these proteins, with the hope of understanding how they work and how they can be exploited in genome editing applications.
